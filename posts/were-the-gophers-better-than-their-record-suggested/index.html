Were the Gophers better than their record suggested?

gophers
bigten
basketball
Author

Tatum Zimmerman

Published

April 22, 2026

At first glance, Minnesota’s season looks easy to explain.

The Gophers struggled in conference play, finishing near the bottom of the Big Ten. Their record at the end of the season suggests that they were never truly competitive. But anyone who watched this team religiously saw something different.

Minnesota played in one of the toughest conferences in college basketball. They lost multiple key players to injury. Yet, despite the losses piling up, many of their games remained close until the final minutes.

So it raises the question: Were the Gophers really as bad as their record shows?

To answer, let’s take a closer look at what happened during the year.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(hoopR)
library(gt)
library(patchwork)

playerboxscores <- load_mbb_player_box(seasons = 2026)

gophers <- playerboxscores |>
 filter(team_name == "Golden Gophers") 

totals <- gophers |> 
  group_by(athlete_display_name) |> 
  summarize(
    avg_assists = mean(assists, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_points = mean(points, na.rm = TRUE),
    avg_rebounds = mean(rebounds, na.rm = TRUE))

injured <- totals |> filter(
athlete_display_name == "Jaylen Crocker-Johnson" |
athlete_display_name == "Robert Vaihola" |
athlete_display_name == "Chansey Willis Jr.")

bar1 <- ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(data=totals |> filter(avg_assists >0), aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_assists),weight=avg_assists,), fill="grey") + 
  geom_bar(data=injured, aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_assists), weight=avg_assists), fill="#7A0019")+ 
  coord_flip() + 
  labs(
    x="", 
    y="Assists Per Game"
    ) + 
  theme_minimal()

bar2 <- ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(data=totals |> filter(avg_points >0), aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_points),weight=avg_points,), fill="grey")+ geom_bar(data=injured, aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_points), weight=avg_points), fill="#7A0019") +
  coord_flip() + 
  labs(
    x="", 
    y="Points Per Game"
    ) + 
  theme_minimal()

bar3 <- ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(data=totals |> filter(avg_rebounds >0), aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_rebounds), weight=avg_rebounds),fill="grey")  +
geom_bar(data=injured, aes(x=reorder(athlete_display_name, avg_rebounds), weight=avg_rebounds), fill="#7A0019") + 
  coord_flip() + 
  labs(
    x="",

bar1 + bar2 + bar3 +
  plot_annotation(
    title = "Minnesota lost major production to injuries",
    subtitle = "Vaihola, Crocker-Johnson, and Willis Jr, who were top contributors in points, assists and rebounds missed time.",
    caption = "Source: HoopR | By Tatum Zimmerman"

    plot.title = element_text(size = 16, face = "bold"),

Availability mattered as much as performance for the Gophers this season.

Minnesota lost several dominant players to injury throughout the year, forcing lineup changes and interrupting consistency. Losing experienced contributors meant younger players were asked to step into bigger roles earlier than expected.

In a conference as competitive as the Big Ten, that kind of inconsistency makes it difficult to turn close games into wins.

Minnesota’s offense showed glimmers of hope, but mistakes limited its potential.

The Gophers ranked well in assists compared to Big Ten teams, with strong ball movement and team chemistry. At the same time, turnovers remained a problem, giving the opposing team too many chances.

That combination made it hard for their offensive strengths to show up on the scoreboard consistently.

Despite their record, the Gophers remained competitive in many tough matchups.

Minnesota played a large number of games that were decided by only a handful of possessions. Instead of being constantly blown out, they were often within reach late in games, but were unable to close them out.

Over the season, those narrow losses made the year look worse on paper than it actually was. Minnesota’s record only tells part of the story.

Injuries made it difficult for the team to stay consistent, games slipped away late, and turnovers stopped their offense from creating momentum. Together, those factors made the season appear far worse than the team actually played.

The numbers suggest the Gophers weren’t as far away from improvement as their record might make it seem.
